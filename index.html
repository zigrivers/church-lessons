When Plan A Breaks

Ruth, Hannah, Samuel, and trusting God when life changes.

Plan faithfully. Hold plans humbly. Follow Christ anyway.

Read each scripture block aloud, tap through the context, then discuss the question before moving on.

Start Here

How to use this page: choose a reader for the scripture verses, choose a second reader for the key information, pause for the discussion question, then move to the next card.

Nobody has to share personal details they do not want to share. The purpose is to understand the stories and notice what God teaches through changed plans, waiting, revelation, reverence, and redemption.

Opening reflection

Think about your life 5, 10, 20, and 30 years from now. What are you hoping for? What parts can you mostly control? What parts require trust?

As we read, watch for what Ruth, Naomi, Boaz, Hannah, Samuel, Eli, and Israel could not control—and what they could still choose.

Ruth: Faith, Loyalty, and Redemption When the Future Falls Apart

Ruth 1 shows loss and changed plans. Ruth 2 shows faithful action and quiet providence. Ruth 3 shows covenant trust and integrity. Ruth 4 shows redemption and a future bigger than Ruth could see.

R1 • Ruth 1:1–5

Plan A breaks: famine, Moab, and loss

Core35 / 45 / 60

Read aloud

1 Now it came to pass in the days when the judges ruled, that there was a famine in the land. And a certain man of Beth-lehem-judah went to sojourn in the country of Moab, he, and his wife, and his two sons.

2 And the name of the man was Elimelech, and the name of his wife Naomi, and the name of his two sons Mahlon and Chilion, Ephrathites of Beth-lehem-judah. And they came into the country of Moab, and continued there.

3 And Elimelech Naomi’s husband died; and she was left, and her two sons.

4 And they took them wives of the women of Moab; the name of the one was Orpah, and the name of the other Ruth: and they dwelled there about ten years.

5 And Mahlon and Chilion died also both of them; and the woman was left of her two sons and her husband.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

Ruth opens during the time of the judges. Naomi’s family leaves Bethlehem because of famine, lives in Moab for about ten years, and then Naomi loses her husband and both sons.

What is happening?

Naomi, Ruth, and Orpah are not starting a sweet romance story. They are starting from hunger, displacement, death, and uncertainty.

Key information
  • The phrase “days when the judges ruled” places the story in an unstable period marked by disorder, idolatry, and foreign oppression.
  • Bethlehem means “house of bread,” so a famine there is an ironic and painful opening.
  • Moab was east of the Dead Sea. Moabites were related to Israelites, but the relationship between the peoples was complicated and often hostile.
  • For widows in this world, losing husbands and sons was not only emotional devastation; it could threaten survival, inheritance, protection, and social standing.
What modern readers might miss

Modern readers may hear “they moved to Moab” like a simple relocation. It was more like leaving covenant land for enemy-neighbor territory because survival was at stake.

Hidden detail

Naomi’s name means “pleasant,” but by verse 5 her life feels anything but pleasant. The story begins with a name and a reality that no longer match.

Why it matters

Ruth’s later loyalty matters more when you understand she is choosing faith in a situation with no obvious safe option.

Discuss

What part of Naomi and Ruth’s situation was outside their control? What choices were still available to them?

Connect it to your life

Teenagers may not face famine in Bethlehem, but they do know what it feels like when family, school, health, friendships, or future plans suddenly change.

Plan vs. Path

What might Ruth’s 5-year plan have looked like before her husband died? What parts of that plan were now underlined as outside her control?

Teacher note

Do not rush past the grief. Let students feel that this is not an inspirational quote pasted onto a comfortable life; Ruth’s faith begins in real loss.

R2 • Ruth 1:8–9, 14

Naomi releases them; Orpah leaves in grief

Extra depth60

Read aloud

8 And Naomi said unto her two daughters in law, Go, return each to her mother’s house: the Lord deal kindly with you, as ye have dealt with the dead, and with me.

9 The Lord grant you that ye may find rest, each of you in the house of her husband. Then she kissed them; and they lifted up their voice, and wept.

14 And they lifted up their voice, and wept again: and Orpah kissed her mother in law; but Ruth clave unto her.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

Naomi hears the famine has ended in Judah and begins returning home. Ruth and Orpah initially go with her, but Naomi urges them to go back to Moab and rebuild their lives.

What is happening?

Naomi is not trying to be cruel. She believes she has no realistic future to offer them.

Key information
  • Naomi asks the Lord to deal kindly with Ruth and Orpah. She recognizes their love and sacrifice.
  • Orpah should not be treated as a villain. She weeps, loves Naomi, and makes a painful but reasonable choice.
  • Ruth’s choice becomes extraordinary not because Orpah is bad, but because Ruth chooses costly loyalty when she has every reason to leave.
What modern readers might miss

Modern readers often flatten the story into “Ruth good, Orpah bad.” The text is more compassionate than that.

Hidden detail

The word “clave” means held fast. Ruth does not drift with Naomi; she grips the relationship and refuses to abandon her.

Why it matters

This helps teens avoid judging people too quickly when they face hard choices we do not fully understand.

Discuss

How can we honor Ruth’s courage without shaming Orpah?

Connect it to your life

Sometimes two sincere people make different decisions under pressure. Mature discipleship avoids easy labels.

Plan vs. Path

When someone else’s decision changes your life, what can you still choose?

Teacher note

This is a good place to model careful reading: the story celebrates Ruth without mocking Orpah.

R3 • Ruth 1:16–18

“Thy people shall be my people”

Core35 / 45 / 60

Read aloud

16 And Ruth said, Entreat me not to leave thee, or to return from following after thee: for whither thou goest, I will go; and where thou lodgest, I will lodge: thy people shall be my people, and thy God my God:

17 Where thou diest, will I die, and there will I be buried: the Lord do so to me, and more also, if ought but death part thee and me.

18 When she saw that she was stedfastly minded to go with her, then she left speaking unto her.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

Naomi tells Ruth to follow Orpah back to Moab. Ruth answers with one of the strongest statements of loyalty in scripture.

What is happening?

Ruth chooses Naomi, Naomi’s people, and Naomi’s God without knowing what will happen next.

Key information
  • Ruth’s statement is more than family affection. It sounds like covenant commitment: people, place, God, death, and future all become tied to Naomi’s covenant world.
  • Ruth is a Moabite choosing Israel’s God. That is a major spiritual and social step.
  • She does not know Boaz, Obed, David, or the future ending. She only knows the next faithful choice.
What modern readers might miss

This is often quoted at weddings, but in context it is spoken by a widowed foreign daughter-in-law to a grieving mother-in-law on a dangerous road.

Hidden detail

Ruth names the Lord in her oath. She is not only attached to Naomi emotionally; she is binding herself spiritually.

Why it matters

Faith is often choosing God before the ending is visible, not after everything is safe.

Discuss

What would it mean for a teenager today to say, “Thy God shall be my God”?

Connect it to your life

This could mean choosing faith when friends drift, choosing covenants when it costs popularity, or staying loyal to someone who is grieving.

Plan vs. Path

Ruth cannot control her past losses or future security. She can still circle loyalty, courage, and God.

Teacher note

Ask students to name what Ruth does not know yet. The list should be long. That is the point.

R4 • Ruth 1:20–22

Naomi feels empty, but harvest is beginning

Recommended45 / 60

Read aloud

20 And she said unto them, Call me not Naomi, call me Mara: for the Almighty hath dealt very bitterly with me.

21 I went out full, and the Lord hath brought me home again empty: why then call ye me Naomi, seeing the Lord hath testified against me, and the Almighty hath afflicted me?

22 So Naomi returned, and Ruth the Moabitess, her daughter in law, with her, which returned out of the country of Moab: and they came to Beth-lehem in the beginning of barley harvest.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

Ruth and Naomi arrive in Bethlehem. The city notices them, and Naomi gives voice to how bitter and empty she feels.

What is happening?

Naomi is honest about pain, while the narrator quietly points to coming provision: barley harvest has begun.

Key information
  • Naomi means pleasant. Mara means bitter. Naomi’s new name expresses grief, not rebellion that we should criticize too quickly.
  • Barley harvest means there is food in the fields. Naomi cannot see the whole future yet, but the story hints that mercy is already starting.
  • Ruth, the Moabite outsider, is still with her. Naomi says she is empty, but the reader can see she is not entirely alone.
What modern readers might miss

Teenagers may skip the harvest detail as background scenery. It is actually a quiet signal that the story is turning.

Hidden detail

Hope enters the story before Naomi feels hopeful. That happens in real life too.

Why it matters

God may be working before we can emotionally recognize it.

Discuss

Why does it matter that the story gives a hint of hope before Naomi feels hopeful?

Connect it to your life

You can be faithful and still not feel okay yet. Feeling hope can lag behind the first signs of help.

Plan vs. Path

When your plan breaks, what signs of help might be easy to miss because you still feel empty?

Teacher note

This is a strong compassion moment. Avoid correcting Naomi’s grief. Invite students to notice both honesty and hidden hope.

R5 • Ruth 2:1–3

Ruth takes the next faithful step

Recommended45 / 60

Read aloud

1 And Naomi had a kinsman of her husband’s, a mighty man of wealth, of the family of Elimelech; and his name was Boaz.

2 And Ruth the Moabitess said unto Naomi, Let me now go to the field, and glean ears of corn after him in whose sight I shall find grace. And she said unto her, Go, my daughter.

3 And she went, and came, and gleaned in the field after the reapers: and her hap was to light on a part of the field belonging unto Boaz, who was of the kindred of Elimelech.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

After arriving at barley harvest, Ruth asks to glean in the fields so she and Naomi can eat.

What is happening?

Ruth acts. She does not know the ending, but she knows the next humble thing she can do.

Key information
  • Gleaning was part of Israel’s law of mercy: portions of the harvest were left for the poor, strangers, fatherless, and widows.
  • Ruth is both a widow and a foreigner, so gleaning fits her vulnerable situation.
  • The phrase “her hap” means it looked like chance that she came to Boaz’s field, but the story invites us to watch for quiet providence.
What modern readers might miss

Trusting God is not the same as doing nothing. Ruth trusts God while also working hard.

Hidden detail

Boaz is introduced before Ruth meets him. The reader knows there is a possible redeemer nearby before Ruth does.

Why it matters

Sometimes God’s guidance is not dramatic. It may look like one faithful step, one field, one conversation.

Discuss

What is the difference between trusting God and doing nothing?

Connect it to your life

For teens, the next faithful step might be apologizing, studying, applying, praying, asking for help, showing up, or serving quietly.

Plan vs. Path

Ruth cannot control the whole future. She can still circle effort, humility, and courage today.

Teacher note

Let students identify “small faithful steps” in real teen life before you give examples.

R6 • Ruth 2:8–12

Boaz protects Ruth: “under whose wings”

Core35 / 45 / 60

Read aloud

8 Then said Boaz unto Ruth, Hearest thou not, my daughter? Go not to glean in another field, neither go from hence, but abide here fast by my maidens:

9 Let thine eyes be on the field that they do reap, and go thou after them: have I not charged the young men that they shall not touch thee? and when thou art athirst, go unto the vessels, and drink of that which the young men have drawn.

10 Then she fell on her face, and bowed herself to the ground, and said unto him, Why have I found grace in thine eyes, that thou shouldest take knowledge of me, seeing I am a stranger?

11 And Boaz answered and said unto her, It hath fully been shewed me, all that thou hast done unto thy mother in law since the death of thine husband: and how thou hast left thy father and thy mother, and the land of thy nativity, and art come unto a people which thou knewest not heretofore.

12 The Lord recompense thy work, and a full reward be given thee of the Lord God of Israel, under whose wings thou art come to trust.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

Boaz notices Ruth, learns who she is, and gives instructions that protect her dignity and safety in the field.

What is happening?

Boaz uses his position to protect rather than exploit. Ruth receives grace as a stranger.

Key information
  • Boaz calls Ruth “my daughter,” speaks respectfully, and makes sure she has safe access to food and water.
  • Ruth asks why he would notice her because she is a stranger. She knows she does not have automatic social standing in Bethlehem.
  • The image of coming under the Lord’s wings means refuge, protection, and covenant belonging.
What modern readers might miss

Boaz’s kindness is not just romantic. It is righteous power used to make a vulnerable person safer.

Hidden detail

Boaz has heard about Ruth’s loyalty before he ever speaks to her. Quiet faithfulness travels farther than Ruth may have realized.

Why it matters

This card teaches both sides: Ruth’s faith in action and Boaz’s Christlike way of helping.

Discuss

How can you help someone in a way that protects their dignity instead of making them feel like a project?

Connect it to your life

A Boaz-like teen might use social influence to include, protect, speak kindly, or quietly make someone’s life safer.

Plan vs. Path

How does Boaz become part of someone else’s changed plan in a righteous way?

Teacher note

Keep the focus on covenant kindness, not dating. Boaz’s first major role is protector and provider.

R7 • Ruth 2:17–20

Naomi starts to see kindness again

Extra depth60

Read aloud

17 So she gleaned in the field until even, and beat out that she had gleaned: and it was about an ephah of barley.

18 And she took it up, and went into the city: and her mother in law saw what she had gleaned: and she brought forth, and gave to her that she had reserved after she was sufficed.

19 And her mother in law said unto her, Where hast thou gleaned to day? and where wroughtest thou? blessed be he that did take knowledge of thee. And she shewed her mother in law with whom she had wrought, and said, The man’s name with whom I wrought to day is Boaz.

20 And Naomi said unto her daughter in law, Blessed be he of the Lord, who hath not left off his kindness to the living and to the dead. And Naomi said unto her, The man is near of kin unto us, one of our next kinsmen.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

Boaz quietly gives Ruth extra grain. Ruth brings food home, and Naomi recognizes both material help and a possible path toward redemption.

What is happening?

Naomi begins to see that the Lord has not abandoned the living or the dead.

Key information
  • An ephah of barley was a large amount for one day of gleaning. Ruth comes home with evidence of unusual generosity.
  • A “next kinsman” or redeemer had the right or responsibility to protect family land, inheritance, and name.
  • Naomi’s language shifts from bitterness to blessing. Her circumstances are not fully solved, but her eyes are opening.
What modern readers might miss

Naomi’s faith does not snap back instantly. It begins with noticing one act of kindness.

Hidden detail

The phrase “to the living and to the dead” shows that redemption in Ruth involves more than Ruth’s personal future. It touches family memory, inheritance, and loss.

Why it matters

Sometimes hope returns through specific evidence, not vague optimism.

Discuss

What changed in Naomi between chapter 1 and chapter 2?

Connect it to your life

When someone is grieving, one concrete act of help can matter more than a dozen cheerful slogans.

Plan vs. Path

What evidence of God’s kindness might you need to learn to notice, even while your plan is still unresolved?

Teacher note

Invite students to think of “small evidence” without requiring personal disclosure.

R8 • Ruth 3:8–13

Ruth asks Boaz to act as redeemer

Core35 / 45 / 60

Read aloud

8 And it came to pass at midnight, that the man was afraid, and turned himself: and, behold, a woman lay at his feet.

9 And he said, Who art thou? And she answered, I am Ruth thine handmaid: spread therefore thy skirt over thine handmaid; for thou art a near kinsman.

10 And he said, Blessed be thou of the Lord, my daughter: for thou hast shewed more kindness in the latter end than at the beginning, inasmuch as thou followedst not young men, whether poor or rich.

11 And now, my daughter, fear not; I will do to thee all that thou requirest: for all the city of my people doth know that thou art a virtuous woman.

12 And now it is true that I am thy near kinsman: howbeit there is a kinsman nearer than I.

13 Tarry this night, and it shall be in the morning, that if he will perform unto thee the part of a kinsman, well; let him do the kinsman’s part: but if he will not do the part of a kinsman to thee, then will I do the part of a kinsman to thee, as the Lord liveth: lie down until the morning.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

Naomi makes a plan for Ruth to appeal to Boaz at the threshing floor. Ruth follows the counsel and makes a direct request.

What is happening?

Ruth asks Boaz to spread his covering over her because he is a near kinsman. Boaz responds with honor, not exploitation.

Key information
  • This ancient scene is easy to misunderstand if we read it through modern dating assumptions.
  • The request to spread his skirt, cloak, or wing over her is a bold appeal for covenant protection and redemption.
  • The language connects back to Ruth 2:12: Ruth came under the Lord’s wings; now Boaz can become an earthly agent of that protection.
  • Boaz could have taken advantage of Ruth’s vulnerability. Instead, he protects her reputation and follows the lawful order.
What modern readers might miss

The point is not scandal or romance first. The point is covenant appeal, vulnerability, courage, and integrity.

Hidden detail

Boaz says Ruth is known as a virtuous woman. Her character has become public even though she entered Bethlehem as an outsider.

Why it matters

Integrity matters most when another person is vulnerable and no crowd is watching.

Discuss

Why is integrity especially important when someone else is vulnerable?

Connect it to your life

For teens, this might mean protecting someone’s reputation, respecting boundaries, refusing to use private information, or doing the right thing when it would be easy not to.

Plan vs. Path

Ruth acts courageously, but then she has to wait on someone else’s agency and timing. Where does your future require that kind of trust?

Teacher note

Be clear and reverent here. Avoid joking about the threshing floor. The better teen application is asking for help and acting with integrity.

R9 • Ruth 4:7–10, 13–17

Boaz redeems; Naomi is restored

Core35 / 45 / 60

Read aloud

7 Now this was the manner in former time in Israel concerning redeeming and concerning changing, for to confirm all things; a man plucked off his shoe, and gave it to his neighbour: and this was a testimony in Israel.

9 And Boaz said unto the elders, and unto all the people, Ye are witnesses this day, that I have bought all that was Elimelech’s, and all that was Chilion’s and Mahlon’s, of the hand of Naomi.

10 Moreover Ruth the Moabitess, the wife of Mahlon, have I purchased to be my wife, to raise up the name of the dead upon his inheritance, that the name of the dead be not cut off from among his brethren, and from the gate of his place: ye are witnesses this day.

13 So Boaz took Ruth, and she was his wife: and when he went in unto her, the Lord gave her conception, and she bare a son.

14 And the women said unto Naomi, Blessed be the Lord, which hath not left thee this day without a kinsman, that his name may be famous in Israel.

15 And he shall be unto thee a restorer of thy life, and a nourisher of thine old age: for thy daughter in law, which loveth thee, which is better to thee than seven sons, hath born him.

17 And the women her neighbours gave it a name, saying, There is a son born to Naomi; and they called his name Obed: he is the father of Jesse, the father of David.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

Boaz goes to the city gate, handles the matter publicly, and redeems the family line after the nearer kinsman declines.

What is happening?

Redemption brings land, family, name, protection, and future back into Naomi and Ruth’s story.

Key information
  • The city gate functioned like a public legal and community space.
  • The sandal custom confirmed a transaction in that culture.
  • To redeem means to buy back, rescue, restore, or reclaim what might otherwise be lost.
  • The women say Ruth is better to Naomi than seven sons—a massive statement in a world where sons were tied to family security and inheritance.
What modern readers might miss

Redemption does not erase the graves in chapter 1. Naomi’s husband and sons are still gone. But God brings life and future into real loss.

Hidden detail

Obed is called a son born to Naomi, even though Ruth gives birth. The community sees Naomi’s emptiness being answered through Ruth’s covenant loyalty.

Why it matters

This helps youth see redemption as restoration through real pain, not pretending pain never happened.

Discuss

What has God restored in Ruth 4? What has He not simply erased?

Connect it to your life

Christlike redemption often looks like protecting, restoring, including, and giving someone a future when they cannot create one alone.

Plan vs. Path

How does Ruth 4 show that God can make more of faithful choices than people can see at the time?

Teacher note

Use Boaz as a limited pointer to Christ, not a perfect symbol. Jesus Christ is the true Redeemer.

R10 • Ruth 4:18–22

The story points beyond what Ruth could see

Recommended45 / 60

Read aloud

18 Now these are the generations of Pharez: Pharez begat Hezron,

19 And Hezron begat Ram, and Ram begat Amminadab,

20 And Amminadab begat Nahshon, and Nahshon begat Salmon,

21 And Salmon begat Boaz, and Boaz begat Obed,

22 And Obed begat Jesse, and Jesse begat David.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

The book ends with a genealogy. That may look boring, but it reveals the larger meaning of Ruth’s ordinary faithfulness.

What is happening?

Ruth, the Moabite outsider, becomes part of the family line that leads to King David and, through David’s line, points forward to Jesus Christ.

Key information
  • Ancient genealogies were not filler. They told readers where a person or family fit in God’s larger story.
  • Ruth could not have known in chapter 1 that her loyalty would connect to David’s family line.
  • The New Testament genealogy of Jesus includes Ruth, showing her place in the story leading to Christ.
What modern readers might miss

A list of names can be the spiritual payoff. God was doing more than Ruth could see.

Hidden detail

The book starts with death and ends with birth and lineage.

Why it matters

Your faithful choices may matter beyond the timeline you can imagine.

Discuss

Why might God’s biggest work in our lives be bigger than what we can see right now?

Connect it to your life

A teen’s quiet choices—faith, loyalty, repentance, service—can bless future people they have not met yet.

Plan vs. Path

Ruth did not know the ending when she chose faith. What would change if you did not require the ending before making the next faithful choice?

Teacher note

This is the best place to connect Ruth to Jesus Christ explicitly.

1 Samuel 1–7: Prayer, Revelation, Reverence, and Returning to the Lord

Hannah’s private prayer leads into Samuel’s public prophetic role. Samuel’s growth contrasts with Eli’s sons, and Israel learns that sacred objects cannot replace repentance and trust in the Lord.

S1 • 1 Samuel 1:3–8

Hannah’s repeated pain at worship

Recommended45 / 60

Read aloud

3 And this man went up out of his city yearly to worship and to sacrifice unto the Lord of hosts in Shiloh. And the two sons of Eli, Hophni and Phinehas, the priests of the Lord, were there.

5 But unto Hannah he gave a worthy portion; for he loved Hannah: but the Lord had shut up her womb.

6 And her adversary also provoked her sore, for to make her fret, because the Lord had shut up her womb.

7 And as he did so year by year, when she went up to the house of the Lord, so she provoked her; therefore she wept, and did not eat.

8 Then said Elkanah her husband to her, Hannah, why weepest thou? and why eatest thou not? and why is thy heart grieved? am not I better to thee than ten sons?

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

Elkanah has two wives, Hannah and Peninnah. Peninnah has children; Hannah has none. Their family goes yearly to worship at Shiloh.

What is happening?

Hannah’s pain is repeated, public, and misunderstood, even by someone who loves her.

Key information
  • Shiloh was an important worship center before the temple in Jerusalem.
  • In Hannah’s society, infertility carried deep social and emotional weight. This is about longing, comparison, shame, identity, and powerlessness—not only wanting a baby.
  • Elkanah loves Hannah, but his question shows that love does not always mean full understanding.
What modern readers might miss

Hannah’s sorrow is happening at a religious place and during a religious routine. Faithful people can hurt deeply at church.

Hidden detail

The phrase “year by year” means this was not one bad day. It was repeated disappointment.

Why it matters

This helps youth connect Hannah to any righteous longing that is painful and slow: family peace, answers, healing, belonging, testimony, or being understood.

Discuss

Why is it hard when people who love us still do not fully understand what hurts?

Connect it to your life

A teen may feel overlooked, behind, compared, or misunderstood even while doing the right things.

Plan vs. Path

What part of Hannah’s life plan was outside her control? What could she still choose?

Teacher note

Name infertility carefully. Do not make the application only about future marriage or children. Broaden it to righteous longing and pain.

S2 • 1 Samuel 1:9–18

Hannah pours out her soul and leaves with peace

Core35 / 45 / 60

Read aloud

10 And she was in bitterness of soul, and prayed unto the Lord, and wept sore.

11 And she vowed a vow, and said, O Lord of hosts, if thou wilt indeed look on the affliction of thine handmaid, and remember me, and not forget thine handmaid, but wilt give unto thine handmaid a man child, then I will give him unto the Lord all the days of his life, and there shall no razor come upon his head.

13 Now Hannah, she spake in her heart; only her lips moved, but her voice was not heard: therefore Eli thought she had been drunken.

15 And Hannah answered and said, No, my lord, I am a woman of a sorrowful spirit: I have drunk neither wine nor strong drink, but have poured out my soul before the Lord.

17 Then Eli answered and said, Go in peace: and the God of Israel grant thee thy petition that thou hast asked of him.

18 And she said, Let thine handmaid find grace in thy sight. So the woman went her way, and did eat, and her countenance was no more sad.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

Hannah goes to the temple area in Shiloh and prays with unusual intensity. Eli sees her mouth moving and misunderstands her.

What is happening?

Hannah does not fake spirituality. She brings sorrow directly to God, receives a priestly blessing, and leaves changed before her circumstances change.

Key information
  • A vow was a serious act of devotion, not a casual promise. Hannah offers her deepest desired blessing back to the Lord.
  • “No razor” points to special consecration, similar to Nazarite devotion.
  • Eli misunderstands Hannah’s silent prayer, which means one of the most faithful moments of her life is misread by someone else.
  • Samuel has not been born in verse 18. Peninnah has not changed. Hannah’s countenance changes before the answer arrives.
What modern readers might miss

Prayer does not always change the situation first. Sometimes it changes the person’s strength, peace, and ability to keep walking.

Hidden detail

Hannah says she poured out her soul. She does not bring God a polished version of herself.

Why it matters

This gives teens permission to pray honestly when they are not okay.

Discuss

What can prayer change before it changes our circumstances?

Connect it to your life

Honest prayer can include complaint, grief, confusion, longing, and trust. It does not have to sound like a talk in sacrament meeting.

Plan vs. Path

What part of your future do you need to bring honestly to God instead of performing confidence?

Teacher note

This is the emotional center of Hannah’s story. Do not turn it into “pray hard enough and get exactly what you want.”

Hannah gives Samuel to the Lord

Read aloud

26 And she said, Oh my lord, as thy soul liveth, my lord, I am the woman that stood by thee here, praying unto the Lord.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

The Lord remembers Hannah, Samuel is born, and after he is weaned Hannah brings him to Shiloh as she promised.

What is happening?

Hannah keeps her vow. The long-awaited blessing is consecrated back to God.

Key information
  • “Lent him to the Lord” does not mean God borrows Samuel temporarily. It means Samuel is dedicated to the Lord’s service.
  • This is deeply sacrificial. Hannah gives God not only her pain, but also her blessing.
  • Samuel’s life as a prophet begins with Hannah’s consecration. Her private prayer becomes part of Israel’s public future.
What modern readers might miss

We often ask God to remove pain. Hannah also teaches what it means to give the blessing back to God once it comes.

Hidden detail

Hannah returns to the same place where she was misunderstood. The location of her sorrow becomes the location of her consecration.

Why it matters

Consecration means God gets access to our successes, not just our problems.

Discuss

What does it mean to give God not just our problems, but also our blessings?

Connect it to your life

A teen can consecrate talents, opportunities, friendships, education, work, and future plans to bless others, not just to build status.

Plan vs. Path

What blessing in your future would you want to use for God instead of only for yourself?

Teacher note

This card connects Hannah to Samuel’s later calling. Her story matters beyond her own household.

After dedicating Samuel, Hannah prays a song of praise. She does not only rejoice in receiving a child; she rejoices in the Lord Himself.

Hannah testifies that God can lift the lowly and reverse human expectations.

  • “Horn” is a symbol of strength or power. Hannah is saying her strength is exalted in the Lord.

Hannah’s joy is not shallow. It is worship after years of sorrow and after a real sacrifice.

Her first line is the title of the Come, Follow Me lesson: “My heart rejoiceth in the Lord.”

The deepest joy is not just getting what we wanted; it is knowing the Lord is real, holy, and steady.

Sometimes gratitude grows deeper when it has passed through waiting.

Use this if you want to connect Hannah’s private story to the larger themes of the book.

S5 • 1 Samuel 2:12, 17, 22–26

Sacred things without reverence

Recommended45 / 60

Read aloud

12 Now the sons of Eli were sons of Belial; they knew not the Lord.

17 Wherefore the sin of the young men was very great before the Lord: for men abhorred the offering of the Lord.

24 Nay, my sons; for it is no good report that I hear: ye make the Lord’s people to transgress.

26 And the child Samuel grew on, and was in favour both with the Lord, and also with men.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

While Samuel grows at Shiloh, Eli’s sons Hophni and Phinehas corrupt their priestly responsibilities and treat worship casually.

What is happening?

Samuel’s quiet growth is placed beside the wickedness of people who hold sacred roles but do not know the Lord.

Key information
  • The sons of Eli are priests, but the text says they “knew not the Lord.” A sacred position is not the same as real discipleship.
  • Their behavior makes people hate or despise the offering of the Lord. Bad spiritual leadership can damage people’s desire to worship.
  • Samuel grows in favor with the Lord and with people, creating a sharp contrast with Eli’s sons.
What modern readers might miss

The danger is not only “bad people out there.” It is treating sacred things as ordinary while still using religious language or position.

Hidden detail

Samuel is young, but spiritually growing. Eli’s sons are older and official, but spiritually decaying.

Why it matters

This prepares students for 1 Samuel 4: Israel will later treat the ark as a sacred object without real obedience.

Discuss

What is the danger of having sacred habits, objects, or callings without real reverence?

Connect it to your life

Church attendance, callings, priesthood office, temple recommends, seminary, or family reputation cannot replace an actual relationship with God.

Plan vs. Path

What kind of disciple do you want to become when no one is impressed by your outward religious appearance?

Teacher note

Be careful: do not make Eli’s sons proof that children’s choices are always their parents’ fault. Keep the focus on reverence, agency, and integrity.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

Samuel is serving in the temple area under Eli. The Lord calls him, but Samuel initially thinks Eli is calling.

What is happening?

Samuel has to learn to recognize revelation. Eli, despite his failures, helps Samuel know how to respond.

Key information
  • “The word of the Lord was precious” means revelation was rare or not widely received in that time.
  • Samuel is not spiritually defective because he does not recognize the voice at first. Revelation often has to be learned.
  • The prayer “Speak; for thy servant heareth” is humble, open, and ready to obey.
What modern readers might miss

Teenagers may assume prophets always recognized God instantly. Samuel needed guidance and repetition.

Hidden detail

The Lord calls Samuel by name before Samuel fully understands who is speaking.

Why it matters

This makes revelation feel learnable rather than unreachable.

Discuss

Why might “Speak; for thy servant heareth” be a hard but powerful prayer for someone your age?

Connect it to your life

Learning revelation may involve quiet, repeated attempts, trusted mentors, scriptures, and willingness to act on what you receive.

Plan vs. Path

You do not need to know your whole future. What would change if your plan started with “Speak; for thy servant heareth”?

Teacher note

Ask: What did Samuel do right before he understood everything? He kept responding.

Israel treats the ark like a good-luck charm

Core35 / 45 / 60

Read aloud

3 And when the people were come into the camp, the elders of Israel said, Wherefore hath the Lord smitten us to day before the Philistines? Let us fetch the ark of the covenant of the Lord out of Shiloh unto us, that, when it cometh among us, it may save us out of the hand of our enemies.

5 And when the ark of the covenant of the Lord came into the camp, all Israel shouted with a great shout, so that the earth rang again.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

Israel is defeated by the Philistines. Instead of repenting and seeking the Lord, they bring the ark from Shiloh into battle.

What is happening?

Israel uses a sacred object as if it can force God’s help. The result is disaster.

Key information
  • The ark of the covenant was Israel’s most sacred religious symbol and was treated with great reverence.
  • The mistake was not respecting the ark too much; it was treating the ark as a tool while ignoring real covenant faithfulness.
  • Hophni and Phinehas, already shown as corrupt priests, are with the ark and die in the battle.
What modern readers might miss

A sacred symbol is not a substitute for obedience. Religious appearance cannot manipulate God.

Hidden detail

Israel asks the right-sounding question—“Wherefore hath the Lord smitten us?”—but chooses a shortcut instead of repentance.

Why it matters

This is painfully relevant: people today can treat religious habits as lucky charms while avoiding real discipleship.

Discuss

How might people today treat religion like a good-luck charm?

Connect it to your life

Examples might include praying only to avoid consequences, wearing symbols without living covenants, or assuming Church activity automatically replaces repentance.

What religious habits in your future need to become real discipleship, not just appearance?

Teacher note

Keep the tone self-reflective, not accusatory. This card can sting in a useful way if handled humbly.

The ark among the Philistines and its return

Read aloud

6:12 And the kine took the straight way to the way of Beth-shemesh, and went along the highway, lowing as they went, and turned not aside to the right hand or to the left; and the lords of the Philistines went after them unto the border of Beth-shemesh.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

After Israel loses the ark, the Philistines put it in Dagon’s temple. The Lord shows His power, and eventually the Philistines send the ark back.

What is happening?

God is not helpless just because Israel was unfaithful. The Lord’s power is shown even in enemy territory.

Key information
  • Dagon was a Philistine god. Placing the ark beside Dagon was likely meant to show that Israel’s God had been defeated.
  • Dagon falling before the ark reverses that message: the Lord is not subject to Philistine power or idols.
  • The ark returns without Israel winning it back by military strength. God does not need human manipulation to defend His holiness.
What modern readers might miss

The ark story is not just weird ancient punishment. It teaches that God is holy, sovereign, and not controllable by either Israel or the Philistines.

Hidden detail

The Philistines have to keep setting their god back up. The contrast is almost ironic: a god that needs human help versus the living Lord.

Why it matters

This card bridges Israel’s failure in chapter 4 and Israel’s repentance in chapter 7.

Discuss

What false gods today keep needing us to prop them back up?

Connect it to your life

Modern false gods might include popularity, control, appearance, money, comfort, attention, or achievement.

Plan vs. Path

If one of your future plans became an idol, how would you know?

Teacher note

Use this if the class enjoys the ark narrative. Keep it focused on God’s holiness, not just the strangeness of the story.

Open full chapter

Bridge summary

After the ark returns, twenty years pass. Israel laments after the Lord, and Samuel calls them to real repentance.

What is happening?

This time Israel does not just grab a sacred object. They put away false gods, prepare their hearts, serve the Lord, and ask Samuel to pray.

Key information
  • Returning to the Lord is not only feeling sorry. It includes putting away rival gods and serving the Lord only.
  • Baalim and Ashtaroth were false gods connected with surrounding Canaanite worship.
  • Ebenezer means “stone of help.” It marks memory: “Hitherto hath the Lord helped us.”
  • The story moves from Hannah’s private prayer to Samuel helping lead national repentance. One consecrated life can bless a whole people.
What modern readers might miss

The victory comes through repentance and reliance on God, not through treating the ark as a shortcut.

Hidden detail

Eben-ezer appeared as a battle location in chapter 4 where Israel lost. In chapter 7, the name becomes a testimony that the Lord helped them.

Why it matters

God can transform even the memory of failure into a place of testimony and help.

Discuss

Why do we need our own “Ebenezer” moments where we remember that the Lord has helped us?

Connect it to your life

A teen can mark spiritual memory through journals, prayers, testimony notes, photos, scriptures, or quiet reminders of answered help.

Plan vs. Path

What false god or distraction might you need to put away so your future is built around the Lord?

Teacher note

This is the best final scripture card. It gives a clear invitation: return, put away, serve, remember.

Before You Close This Page

Ruth: Ruth loses her expected future, chooses covenant loyalty, takes humble action, receives kindness and protection, and becomes part of a redemptive story much larger than she could see.

Naomi: Naomi returns empty and bitter, but God’s mercy begins quietly before she recognizes it.

Boaz: Boaz uses his position to protect, bless, and redeem rather than to take advantage.

Hannah: Hannah brings her longing honestly to God, receives peace before the full answer, and consecrates her blessing back to the Lord.

Samuel: Samuel learns to hear the Lord’s voice and grows into a prophet during a spiritually difficult time.

Israel: Israel learns that outward religious symbols cannot replace real trust, reverence, repentance, and obedience.

Jesus Christ: Jesus Christ is the Redeemer. He can walk with us through loss, waiting, uncertainty, correction, repentance, and changed plans. He can make more of our faithful choices than we can see in the moment.
